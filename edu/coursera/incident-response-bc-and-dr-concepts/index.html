Responding to a Malware Alert: A Case Study

Let us look at the response to a malware alert. First, a Security Operations Center (SOC) Analyst receives an alert of anomalous behavior at a workstation. Since the alert is for only one workstation, the SOC Analyst triages the event. Further investigation shows that the alert was for behavior by a known user that did not pose a threat to the organization.

Answer: The incident response plan should be terminated at this point. Since the alert was triggered by known user behavior that doesn't pose a threat, it is categorized as a false positive. The focus should shift to refining detection mechanisms to reduce such occurrences.

Answer: The incident response plan should proceed if the event involves software explicitly disallowed by the organization. This situation requires investigation to understand the scope of policy violation, and potential security risks to apply necessary remediation steps.

Answer: Should the event involve more than one computer, the incident response plan must escalate. This indicates a potential spread of the threat, requiring a broader investigation, containment measures across affected systems, and possibly, activation of business continuity plans if operational impact is significant.

Answer: If the event escalates further, the incident response plan must adapt to the increased severity. This might involve broader team engagement, external expertise, and possibly law enforcement collaboration. Ensuring communication and documenting actions become crucial for post-incident analysis and future preparedness.
